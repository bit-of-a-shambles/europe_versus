## The Rationing Narrative

The criticism goes like this: European "socialized" healthcare means **long waits, rationed care, and government bureaucrats deciding who lives and dies**. Americans, the story goes, have the "best healthcare in the world" because their market-driven system promotes innovation and choice.

What does the data actually show?

## Life Expectancy: The Bottom Line

The single most important healthcare metric is how long people live:

{{metric:life_expectancy}}

**Core EU life expectancy: 82.6 years. United States: 79.3 years.** Europeans live over three years longer on average — and this gap has been *widening* over the past two decades.

But raw life expectancy includes years lived with illness or disability. What about *quality* of those years?

{{metric:healthy_life_expectancy}}

**Core EU healthy life expectancy: 69.8 years. United States: 63.9 years.** Europeans not only live longer — they live nearly **six more healthy years**. This reflects better preventive care, lower chronic disease burden, and more effective management of age-related conditions.

## Maternal and Infant Health

Perhaps the starkest comparison is in outcomes for mothers and babies — where healthcare quality is most directly measured:

{{metric:infant_mortality_rate}}

US infant mortality is **55% higher** than Europe's and **83% higher** than the Core EU. American babies are more likely to die in their first year of life than babies in any Western European country.

{{metric:maternal_mortality_ratio}}

The maternal mortality picture is even more dramatic:

- **Core EU**: 5.4 deaths per 100,000 live births
- **United States**: 21.1 deaths per 100,000 live births

An American mother is **4× more likely to die** from pregnancy-related causes than a mother in Germany, France, or Italy. This figure has been *rising* in the US while falling almost everywhere in Europe.

## Cost: Spending More, Getting Less

The US healthcare system is by far the most expensive in the world:

{{metric:health_expenditure_gdp_percent}}

The United States spends **16.5% of GDP** on healthcare — nearly **80% more** than the European average of 9.2% and **47% more** than even the highest-spending Core EU countries at 11.2%.

What does that extra spending buy? As we've seen: shorter lives, higher infant mortality, higher maternal mortality, and worse chronic disease outcomes.

{{metric:out_of_pocket_health_expenditure}}

Americans also pay significantly more out of pocket — $1,472 per year versus $909 in Europe. And this understates the real burden: American out-of-pocket costs come *on top of* insurance premiums averaging $7,000+ per person per year. Medical debt is the leading cause of personal bankruptcy in the US — a phenomenon that essentially doesn't exist in Europe, where universal coverage means no one faces financial ruin from illness.

## Prevention: The Upstream Advantage

The European model emphasizes prevention over treatment — and it shows in population health outcomes:

{{metric:obesity_prevalence}}

US obesity prevalence: **42.9%**. Core EU: **19.2%**. Europe overall: **24.8%**.

European countries achieve lower obesity rates through:
- **Walkable cities** and cycling infrastructure that encourage daily physical activity
- **Food regulations** that limit processed food ingredients, added sugars, and portion sizes
- **School nutrition standards** and public health campaigns
- **Shorter working hours** that leave time for cooking, exercise, and outdoor life

Lower obesity means less diabetes, less heart disease, less cancer, and less strain on healthcare systems — a virtuous cycle that starts with urban planning and nutrition policy, not hospital beds.

## The "Innovation" Question

Critics correctly note that the US produces breakthrough medical innovations — new drugs, surgical techniques, and medical devices. This is real and valuable. However:

1. Much US medical research is **publicly funded** (NIH budget: ~$50 billion/year)
2. European countries contribute **more scientific publications** overall
3. Innovation in one country **benefits all countries** — Europeans use American advances and vice versa
4. The innovation argument doesn't justify a system where maternal mortality is 4× higher

Innovation and universal coverage are not in tension. Every European country also invests in medical research while simultaneously providing universal care.

## Conclusion

The data is unambiguous: European healthcare systems deliver **better outcomes at lower cost** than the American system. Europeans live longer, have healthier lives, lose fewer mothers and babies, and spend far less — both as a share of GDP and out of their own pockets.

The US system excels in certain specialized areas (cutting-edge cancer treatment, rare disease research). But on the metrics that matter to the average person — *Will I live a long, healthy life? Will my baby survive? Will I go bankrupt if I get sick?* — European systems are measurably superior.

## The Competitiveness Narrative

The argument is familiar: **Europe's climate strategy is economically costly.** Carbon pricing can raise short-term costs for some sectors. Meanwhile, critics argue that the US and China can grow faster with fewer constraints.

This narrative frames environmental policy as purely a cost. But what if it's actually an investment? Let's look at the data.

## Carbon Emissions: Doing More With Less

The most fundamental measure of climate performance is CO₂ emissions per capita:

{{metric:co2_emissions_per_capita}}

- **Europe**: 6.23 tonnes per person
- **Core EU**: 5.58 tonnes per person
- **United States**: 14.2 tonnes per person
- **China**: 8.66 tonnes per person

Europe emits **less than half** the CO₂ per capita of the United States. And this gap has been widening as European emissions fall while US emissions remain stubbornly high.

Crucially, this hasn't come at the expense of prosperity. As our [economic analysis](/facts/economic-growth) shows, Core EU productivity per hour is virtually identical to the US. Europe produces nearly the same economic output per working hour while emitting half the carbon. That's not sacrifice - it's efficiency.

European emissions peaked in the 1970s-80s and have been declining since, driven by:
- Coal phase-outs (UK, Germany, France lowering coal dramatically)
- The EU Emissions Trading System - the world's largest carbon market
- Building efficiency regulations
- Modal shift toward public transport and cycling
- Industrial efficiency improvements

## Renewable Energy: Leading the Transition

The energy transition is perhaps Europe's most visible climate achievement:

{{metric:renewable_energy_share}}

- **Europe**: 43% of electricity from renewables
- **Core EU**: 46% of electricity from renewables
- **United States**: 24% of electricity from renewables
- **China**: 34% of electricity from renewables

Europe generates **nearly twice the share** of electricity from renewables as the United States. This isn't marginal - it's transformative.

And when we include nuclear power (which is carbon-free though not renewable), the picture is even stronger:

{{metric:low_carbon_energy_share}}

- **Europe**: 28% of total primary energy from low-carbon sources
- **Core EU**: 31% of total primary energy from low-carbon sources
- **United States**: 20%

European countries like Denmark, Portugal, and Austria routinely generate 70-100% of their electricity from renewables. Germany - Europe's industrial powerhouse - has gone from near-zero to over 50% renewable electricity in two decades while maintaining its position as the world's third-largest exporter.

**Why does this matter beyond climate?**

1. **Energy security**: Every megawatt of wind or solar power reduces dependence on imported gas from Russia, oil from the Middle East, and coal from global markets. After Russia's invasion of Ukraine in 2022, this strategic dimension became starkly clear.
2. **Price stability**: Renewable energy has zero fuel costs. Once built, the energy is essentially free. This increasingly provides a competitive advantage over fossil-fuel-dependent economies.
3. **Industrial creation**: Europe's renewable energy sector employs over 1.5 million people directly and has created new export industries in wind turbines, solar technology, and grid management.

## Forests: Growing, Not Shrinking

While deforestation accelerates in the Amazon, Southeast Asia, and Central Africa, Europe is doing the opposite:

{{metric:forest_area_share}}

European forest coverage is expanding. EU forest area has increased by roughly 10% since 1990, driven by active reforestation programs, sustainable forestry practices, and the shift away from agriculture in marginal lands.

This matters for three reasons:
1. **Carbon sequestration**: Growing forests absorb CO₂
2. **Biodiversity**: Expanding forests provide habitat
3. **Demonstration**: It proves that economic development can *increase* rather than destroy natural capital

## Economic Performance: Still Thriving

If green policies were destroying Europe's economy, you'd expect to see declining GDP. Let's check:

{{metric:gdp_per_capita_ppp}}

European GDP per capita has continued to grow steadily through decades of increasingly ambitious climate policy. Core EU GDP per capita is among the highest in the world. The Nordics - arguably the most environmentally ambitious countries - are also among the richest.

The relationship between prosperity and environmental performance is not negative - it's *positive*. Rich countries can afford to invest in clean energy, and clean energy makes countries more efficient and competitive.

**The new industrial opportunity**: Europe's early investment in green technology has created first-mover advantages in:
- **Offshore wind**: European companies (Ørsted, Siemens Gamesa, Vestas) hold significant global market share
- **Electric vehicles**: European manufacturers are scaling rapidly
- **Green hydrogen**: Europe leads in electrolyzer technology
- **Carbon capture**: European companies lead in industrial CCUS
- **Circular economy**: European regulations drive recycling, reuse, and sustainable manufacturing

These aren't future hypotheticals - they're existing multi-billion-euro industries employing millions of people.

## Conclusion

Europe has cut per-capita emissions to less than half the US level while maintaining world-class productivity and growing GDP. Renewable energy has reached 43% of electricity generation. Forests are expanding. Green technology has created new export industries employing millions.

Climate policy is reshaping parts of Europe's economy and energy system. Lower fossil-fuel dependence can improve energy security, and export-oriented clean-tech sectors can support competitiveness over time.

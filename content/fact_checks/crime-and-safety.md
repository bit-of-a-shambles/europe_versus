## The Safety Narrative

Scroll through social media or watch cable news and you'll encounter a persistent narrative: **Europe is becoming dangerous.** Crime is rising, cities are unsafe, and terrorism is an ever-present threat.

This narrative rarely comes with data — and for good reason. The data tells an entirely different story.

## Homicide Rates

The most fundamental measure of safety is the homicide rate — intentional killings per 100,000 people:

{{metric:homicide_rate}}

The numbers are stark:

- **Core EU** (Germany, France, Italy, Netherlands, Belgium, Luxembourg): **0.94** per 100,000
- **Europe overall**: **2.2** per 100,000 (pulled up by a few Eastern European outliers)
- **United States**: **5.8** per 100,000

The US homicide rate is roughly **2.6× higher than the European average** and **6× higher than the Core EU**. This isn't a recent divergence — it's been true for decades.

Even Europe's highest rates (in some Eastern European and Balkan countries) are comparable to or lower than the US rate. And the trend across Europe is consistently downward — homicide rates have been falling since the 1990s.

## Gun Violence

Where the contrast becomes most dramatic is in firearm violence:

{{metric:gun_death_rate}}

Europe's firearm homicide rate of **0.14 per 100,000** is **31 times lower** than the US rate of **4.42**. To put this in perspective: the US experiences more firearm homicides in a single week than most European countries experience in an entire year.

This isn't coincidental. European countries broadly implement:
- Strict licensing requirements for gun ownership
- Background checks and waiting periods
- Bans on assault-style weapons and high-capacity magazines
- Registration and tracking of all firearms

The result is that gun violence is vanishingly rare across Europe. Mass shootings, a regular occurrence in the US, are extraordinary events in European countries.

## Incarceration

If high incarceration rates were the answer to crime, the US would be the safest country on Earth. It isn't:

{{metric:incarceration_rate}}

The United States incarcerates **542 people per 100,000** — roughly **4× the European rate** of 136, and **5.6× the Core EU rate** of 97. Yet as we've already seen, the US has far higher rates of violent crime.

European criminal justice systems generally emphasize:
- **Rehabilitation** over punishment
- **Shorter sentences** with focus on reintegration
- **Alternatives to incarceration** (community service, electronic monitoring, restorative justice)
- **Humane prison conditions** that reduce recidivism

The Nordic model is particularly instructive: countries like Norway and Finland have some of the lowest incarceration rates AND lowest crime rates in the world. Their recidivism rates are roughly half of the US rate.

## Terrorism

The fear of terrorism in Europe is real and understandable — but the actual risk is extraordinarily small:

{{metric:terrorism_deaths}}

Terrorism deaths in Europe peaked during the political violence of the 1970s and 1980s (the IRA in the UK, ETA in Spain, the Red Brigades in Italy). Since then, the trend has been dramatically downward despite occasional devastating attacks.

To put the risk in perspective:
- Annual risk of dying in a terrorist attack in Europe: roughly **1 in 10 million**
- Annual risk of dying in a car accident in Europe: roughly **1 in 20,000**
- Annual risk of dying from air pollution in Europe: roughly **1 in 2,000**

Terrorism is a legitimate security concern that European intelligence services work constantly to counter. But it is not, by any statistical measure, a significant source of danger to the average European resident.

## Conclusion

By virtually every measure of violent crime, Europe is among the safest places on Earth — and significantly safer than the United States. Homicide rates are a fraction of US levels. Gun deaths are 31× lower. Incarceration is 4× lower while achieving better safety outcomes.

The "dangerous Europe" narrative doesn't survive contact with the data. It's driven by media attention to spectacular events (terrorism, high-profile crimes) rather than the statistical reality of daily life. An average European is far safer walking their streets than an average American — and the gap has been widening in Europe's favor for decades.
